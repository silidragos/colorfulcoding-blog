 <h1> NOSE, EYES AND EARS: HEAD POSE ESTIMATION BY LOCATING FACIAL KEYPOINTS </h1> 
 <small> by Aryaman
    Gupta, Kalpit Thakkar, Vineet Gandhi and P J Narayanan </small>

 <p><b>Face augmentation</b> is one the most
    mainstream applications of AR. And although it seems a solved solution now, it's still a very interesting and not
    easy problem.
 However powerful Neural networks are, the first intuition to put <b>raw or grayscale images</b>
    directly into a <b>Convolutional Neural Network</b>(<a
        href="https://www.pyimagesearch.com/2017/12/11/image-classification-with-keras-and-deep-learning/"> Learn more
        about theory and their implementation in Keras here</a>) and return the <b>orientation</b> is simply not good
    enough. So how do this guys do it? </p>

    src="/images/4/1.jpg" />

 <h2>First things first! Defining the problem.
    Input and Output.</h2>
 <h3>Input</h3>
 <p>All we have are images (or video frames). They are gathered and
    used for training from:
 <br /> <b>BIWI Kinect Head Pose Database</b>
 <br /> <b>AFLW (Annotated Facial
        Landwarks in the Wild)</b>
 <br /> P.S. I really love about science papers that you find about free
    <b>datasets</b> like these. Goodies!
 </p>
 

<hr />

 <h3>Output</h3>
 <p>
 How do we define head orientation? Well, it's just an Euler rotation X, Y, Z
    or more academically said <b>yaw, pitch and roll.</b>
 </p>

 <h2> Tl;dr What's the solution already?
</h2>
 <p> These guys offer a smart workaround. They put the <b>RGB images</b> through five other <b> Convolutional
        Neural Networks</b> so they can first get the positions of 5 keypoints: <b>Ears, Eyes</b> and <b>Nose </b>.

    These positions have some uncertainty, so they don't get just the (X, Y, Z) position of each, but a <b>2D soft
        localization heatmap image</b> for each one.
 <br />
 The 2D heatmaps can now be brought inside a CNN and
    get the Yaw, Pitch and Roll. Easy peasy right?
 </p>

 
 <img
    src="/images/4/2.jpg" />

 <h2> Implementation details </h2>
 <p> 
 A
    few things that I found a bit more interesting here.
 <br /> 

<ul>
 <li>The uncertainty maps of the 5 keypoints are generated by using <a
            href="https://github.com/CMU-Perceptual-Computing-Lab/openpose">OpenPose</a></li>
 <li>All 5 heatmaps
        are stacked over each other, generating a 5-channel feature map that will be used as input.</li>
 <li> The
        used loss function is basically an Average Least Square between the orientation we got versus the expected one.
    </li>
 <li> It surpasses state-of-the-art techniques. <b> Boom! </b></li>
 </ul>
 
 </p>
